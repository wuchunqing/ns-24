Armstrong Urine Color Scale Reference

The gold-standard field tool for hydration monitoring when lab testing (urine specific gravity / osmolality) is unavailable.

Colors 1–2: Well Hydrated

Pale to very pale yellow. Urine specific gravity typically <1.010. Optimal for training & competition. Maintain current fluid habits.

Colors 3–4: Adequate / Mild

Light yellow to straw. Acceptable for most athletes. Increase fluid 300–500 ml before next session if exercising in heat.

Colors 5–6: Dehydrated

Amber to dark yellow. Performance decrements likely. ACSM: drink 5–7 ml/kg body weight 4 hours before exercise; add electrolytes.

Colors 7–8: Severely Dehydrated

Dark amber to brown/orange. Stop intense exercise. Rehydrate with electrolyte solution. Seek medical care if accompanied by confusion or no urine >8 hrs.

ACSM Body-Weight Dehydration Thresholds (During Exercise)

  1. <1% loss: Minimal — euhydrated to mildly hypohydrated; no action required
  2. 1–2% loss: Mild dehydration — thirst, performance may decline 2–5% (endurance)
  3. 2–3% loss: Significant — KSI red flag for athletes; impaired thermoregulation & cognitive function
  4. >3% loss: Serious — stop exercise, aggressive rehydration, medical evaluation if symptomatic
  5. Rehydration formula: Drink 150% of fluid lost (1.5 × sweat loss) over 2–4 hours with sodium (460–1150 mg/L)

KSI Heat Illness Warning Signs

The Korey Stringer Institute recommends immediate medical removal from activity if an athlete exhibits:

  • Core temp >40°C (104°F) with CNS dysfunction
  • Collapse, confusion, or loss of consciousness
  • >4% body weight loss in single session
  • No urine production for extended period with dark urine color 7–8

Hydration Assessment FAQ

How accurate is the urine color chart?

Armstrong's 8-point scale correlates with urine specific gravity (r ≈ 0.75–0.85) in field conditions. Best used on first morning void or mid-stream sample. Vitamins (B2), beets & certain medications can cause false readings — note these factors.

How do I perform the sweat rate test correctly?

Per ACSM & Runner's World protocols: (1) Void bladder, (2) weigh dry pre-exercise, (3) exercise without drinking initially for accurate loss (or track all intake), (4) towel dry sweat, (5) weigh post-exercise. Sweat rate (L/h) = [(pre − post) + fluid consumed] ÷ hours exercised.

What is the 150% rehydration rule?

ACSM recommends consuming 150% of measured fluid deficit to account for ongoing urine losses during recovery. Spread over 2–4 hours. Include 20–50 mmol/L sodium (460–1150 mg/L) to retain fluid — plain water alone is insufficient after heavy sweating.

How much should I drink daily as an endurance athlete?

Baseline: 30–40 ml/kg body weight/day (ACSM), plus sweat losses. A 70 kg runner needs ~2.1–2.8 L baseline + exercise replacement. Hot environments may push total needs to 4–6 L. Use this checker + sweat rate test for personalization.

Can I be over-hydrated?

Yes — exercise-associated hyponatremia (EAH) occurs from excessive plain water without sodium, especially in long events. Never drink beyond thirst in events <4 hours unless following a proven race nutrition plan. Pair hydration with electrolyte replacement.

When should I check urine color?

KSI & BiteKit recommend: first morning void (best baseline), pre-workout, and post-workout. Consistent color 3+ across multiple days suggests chronic mild dehydration — increase daily fluid by 500 ml and review electrolyte intake.

Pre-Workout Hydration (ACSM)

  • 5–7 ml/kg body weight 4 hrs before exercise
  • Additional 3–5 ml/kg if urine color ≥4
  • Include 200–300 mg sodium in pre-race drink
  • Avoid overdrinking — drink to thirst for events <1 hr
  • Test sweat rate in race conditions

During Exercise Guidelines

  • 200–300 ml every 15–20 min (moderate)
  • Match sweat rate in heat (up to 1–1.5 L/hr)
  • 300–1000 mg sodium/hr for sessions >60 min
  • Weigh before/after to refine plan
  • Cool fluids (10–15°C) improve palatability

Post-Workout Recovery

  • 150% of weight lost in fluids over 2–4 hrs
  • 1.5 g/kg carbs within 30 min post-exercise
  • Sodium: 500–700 mg per recovery liter
  • Monitor next-morning urine color
  • Body weight should return to pre within 24 hrs

Signs You're Under-Hydrated

  • Urine color ≥5 on Armstrong chart
  • >2% body weight drop post-workout
  • Thirst + elevated heart rate at rest
  • Dark circles, sunken eyes (severe)
  • Performance drop vs hydrated baseline
